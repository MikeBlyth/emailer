It has been three years since our last newsletter. While the “code” of our daily lives hasn’t changed much, the version updates have been significant.

The Gift of “Aha” Moments

After a couple of years of working part-time at a great pediatric practice, I officially retired a year ago. I’ve spent much of my time since as a volunteer math, statistics, and computer science tutor with Upchieve (https://www.upchieve.org). This program is a great approach to educational inequality: it provides free, 24/7 online tutoring to low-income middle and high school students.

The highlight for me isn’t just the teaching; it’s that specific moment when a student who has been struggling or feeling far behind suddenly tells me they finally “get it.” Watching a student’s confidence shift after they ace an assignment they thought was impossible is so rewarding. Upchieve is always looking for volunteers, and the schedule is totally open—you just log on when you have a spare 30 minutes. If you are looking for a high-impact way to use some spare time, I’d love to talk to you about it. Check it out at the link above.

Adaptability and Resilience

Barb is finding new ways to stay involved at church, contributing to her Bible study and helping prepare meals for a local homeless center. As for activities, while her vision loss has made reading impossible and writing and cooking more difficult, she is finding ways to cope such as reading lots of audio books. We’ve found a modern workaround for our evening entertainment: Barb uses a Quest VR headset instead of a TV. The lenses help her see films with much more clarity, and we’ve spent many good evenings enjoying shows like The Good Karma Hospital and Miss Scarlet.

Milestones: From Tuscany to California

Last year marked our 50th anniversary. We used it as the perfect excuse for a two-week trip to Italy in April, soaking in the scenery, history and art of Tuscany, Rome, and Milan. In contrast, a more somber milestone was a trip to California this month for a memorial service for Barb’s brother Bob. While it was a sad occasion, being surrounded by family in California provided a meaningful opportunity to reconnect and remember why these relationships are so vital.

Final Thoughts

Like many of you, we have become more than just “politically engaged;” we view the current state of our nation as the crisis of our lifetime. We have been aghast to watch the decline of a stable, law-based democracy and the rise of authoritarianism and the demonization of others. Having lived in authoritarian and failing countries, we recognize these patterns with a visceral clarity and have no desire to see them repeated here. With Lisa as our star encourager, we have channeled this alarm into action—participating in protests, voter mobilization, and advocacy. We urge you to join us in this critical work.

We’ll leave you with a few recommendations that moved us this year:

  • Books: Everything Sad is Untrue by Daniel Nyeri (a beautiful storytelling memoir) and both The Covenant of Water and Cutting for Stone by Abraham Verghese. For a historical perspective on our current climate, we recommend Jesus and John Wayne by Kristin Kobes Du Mez. For fun with a serious twist, the Don Tillman series (starting with The Rosie Project) by Graeme C. Simsion kept us laughing on long road trips.

  • Screen: The Visitor (2007) and The Samuel Project (2018) were two of many standout films for us this year.

Wishing you a year of progress, blessing, “aha” moments, and cats that rock.
